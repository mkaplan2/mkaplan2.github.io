Analysis of Mental Health in the Tech Industry in the United States¶

A data science project by Maya Kaplan

Introduction¶

The objective of this project is to analyse survey data regarding people who work in tech and their relationship to their workplace, mental health, and experiences with how these two areas of life interact with each other. In particular, we will be looking at tech companies within the United States to understand how the United States' tech space is faring in terms of mental health.

Throughout this tutorial, we will attempt to find patterns in how people percieve mental health and their workplace, as well as whether or not these perceptions and experiences differ by gender. We will also look at how these factors relate to mental health treatment.

Data Collection:¶

This is the initial stage in the data life cycle, called "Data Collection". During this stage, data is collected from resources such as downloaded files, apis, and/or websites.

The dataset we will use is obtained from Kaggle. This dataset represents the data from an 2014 survey that measured attitudes of workers in the tech industry regarding mental health. Each of the dataset's columns represent the response to a particular question. To see detailed documentation of what each column represents, check out the documentation regarding this dataset here: https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/osmi/mental-health-in-tech-survey

To begin, we will import the dataset as a csv file, and then use the pd.read_csv function (documentation can be found here: https://pandas.pydata.org/docs/reference/api/pandas.read_csv.html) from pandas to read the csv file and convert it to a dataframe.

But what is a DataFrame exactly?¶

A DataFrame is a structure that holds information similar to the way in which a table holds information. It contains rows and columns, both of which can be manipulated for data collection. Documentation regarding the DataFrame can be found here: https://pandas.pydata.org/docs/reference/api/pandas.DataFrame.html

Data Representation: Looking at the survey participants as a whole¶

In order to compare answers for each question among servey participates, we will use a bar graph for each question of interest. In this case, the y - axis of the bar graph will represent the number of participants that gave a particular answer to a question, whereas the x-axis will show the different answers given for each question.

Generating a bar graph can be done in a multitude of ways. However, in order to write less-repetitive code, it would be of interest to create a function that can be used to generate a bar graph for any question that is inputed as a paramater. This way, we only have to call this function for each question we would like to graph, instead of writing the same code multiple times for each question.

In order to generate our bar graphs we will use the bar() function provided by matplotlib (see documentation here: https://matplotlib.org/3.5.0/api/_as_gen/matplotlib.pyplot.bar.html)

#Function to graph the number of people that answered each answer for particular question
def barGraphGeneral(title, x_column_name, y_axis_label):
    
    x_axis_labels = us_tech[x_column_name].unique()
    
    #dictionary is a dictionary of key-value pairs of: answer (key) and number of times the answer was recorded in the survey (value)
    dictionary = {}
    
    #iterating through the dataframe to populate the dictionary with values
    for index, row in us_tech.iterrows():
        if ( dictionary.get(row[x_column_name]) == None ):
            #If the answer was not yet added to the dictionary, need to initialize to 1
            dictionary[row[x_column_name]] = 1
        else:
            #If already exists, increment the value by one in the dictionary
            dictionary[row[x_column_name]] = dictionary.get(row[x_column_name]) + 1
    
    x_axis_data = list(dictionary.keys())
    y_axis_data = list(dictionary.values())
    
    #Making bar graph with the values from the dictionary
    fig = plt.figure()
    plt.title(title)
    #fig.suptitle(title)
    plt.ylabel(y_axis_label)
    plt.bar(x_axis_data, y_axis_data)
    
    plt.show()

#Function for comparing the men and women answers for a specific question
def pieCompareGenderPercentages(title, x_column_name):
    
    #Getting data for female survey participants
    female_dataframe = us_tech[us_tech["Gender"] == "Female"]
    count_female = female_dataframe[x_column_name].value_counts()
    percentage_female = (count_female/us_tech["Gender"].value_counts()["Female"])*100
    count_and_percentage_female = pd.concat([count_female, percentage_female], axis = 1, keys = ["Count", "Percentage"])

    #Getting data for male survey participants
    male_dataframe = us_tech[us_tech["Gender"] == "Male"]
    count_male = male_dataframe[x_column_name].value_counts()
    percentage_male = (count_male/us_tech["Gender"].value_counts()["Male"])*100
    count_and_percentage_male = pd.concat([count_male, percentage_male], axis = 1, keys = ["Count", "Percentage"])

    fig = plt.figure(figsize=(4,3),dpi=144)

    #Adding pie chart for male data
    ax2 = fig.add_subplot(121)
    ax2.pie(percentage_male, labels = male_dataframe[x_column_name].unique(), autopct='%1.1f%%', startangle=90, radius = 1.2)
    ax2.axis('equal')
    ax2.set_title("Male", y = 0)

    #Adding pie chart for female data
    ax1 = fig.add_subplot(122)
    ax1.pie(percentage_female, labels = female_dataframe[x_column_name].unique(), autopct='%1.1f%%', startangle=90, radius = 1.2)
    ax1.axis('equal')
    ax1.set_title("Female", y = 0)

    fig.suptitle(title)

    plt.show()

Insight and Company Policy Decisions¶


  In our observations of males vs. female responses to different questions, we noticed a number of differences. For example, we noticed that for both mental and physical issues, females were more likely to think that sharing issues would lead to negative consequences. Females also were more likely to share mental health issues with coworkers, but less likely than males to share with supervisors. This may be for a number of reasons. For example, this may be because females are more worried about the repercussions of sharing this information with a supervisor. This also might be true because tech companies have male-dominated leadership roles, so females might be less inclined to share their issues with employers, as discrimination is not unheard of.
  We also observed that females were more likely to believe that their employer takes mental health as seriously as physical heath, whereas males were more likely to not know their employer's stance. This may be for a number of reasons, including that males might not be as interested in their companies' stance, naturally making them less likely to be informed on their companies' stance. It is also possible that men are less likely to value mental health in relation to their company (as men have been conditioned to be less emotionally aware and more alexithymic by society), and therefore be more likely to work at companies where the company has not stated their stance on mental health.
  Overall, from each answered question, it can be observed that there is a large portion of employees who believe that mental health is not taken as seriously in the work place as physical health. It can also be observed that overall, there is a large portion of employees who believe that sharing mental health issues could possibly, or even definitely, create negative consequences for the workers.
  In addition, there seemed to be a lot of variance amoung the workers who have gotten mental health treatment, and therefore the models used to predict whether a worker has gotten treatment were not completely accurate. In fact, the most accurate model was the Logistic Regression model, which only had an accuracy of around 75.6 percent.



From these insights, we can make a few suggestions about how tech companies in the United States can do better to help workers when it comes to mental health:
  1. As quite a lot of workers do not konw their companies stance on mental health, companies should consider making it a point ot educate their workers on the mental health resources they have for their workers to utilize. In addition, if they do not have mental health resources available, they should implement programs to help workers with mental health.
  2. Individual managers should also consider talking to their workers during one-on-one meetings (perhaps during onboarding or during the start of working at the company) to inform them on where they stand on mental health, and whether or not they are willing to be a resource to the workers.
